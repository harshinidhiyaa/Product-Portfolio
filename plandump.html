PlanDump AI

Type Original Product Concept
Status Concept + Prototype in Progress
Problem Space Social Coordination
Target User Gen Z Friend Groups

Group chats are where weekend plans go to die. Someone drops an Instagram Reel. Someone else sends a Zomato link. A third person shares a Google Maps pin. And then — silence. PlanDump AI converts that fragmented chaos into three ready-to-share itineraries in under 30 seconds.

1. The Problem

Group plans don't fall apart because of a lack of ideas. They fall apart because nobody wants to be the person who does the work of turning ideas into a plan.

… no reply for 2 days. Plans fall through. Everyone ends up at the same cafe they always go to.

The problem isn't information. Everyone has ideas. The problem is that converting fragmented links into a real, logistically viable plan requires one person to voluntarily absorb all the coordination cost — cross-referencing distances, checking operating hours, figuring out what order makes sense, and proposing something concrete enough for the group to commit to.

2. The Insight

The planner is doing unpaid cognitive labor for the group.
Planning isn't an information problem. It's a coordination problem — and the cost falls on one person every time.

Most tools try to solve this by giving users more information: better maps, richer reviews, smarter filters. PlanDump solves it differently — by eliminating the decision entirely and handing the group three concrete options they just have to vote on.

3. User Research

To validate the problem, I spoke with five people between the ages of 21 and 26 who regularly try to coordinate weekend plans in group chats. The core question: "Tell me about the last time your group failed to make a plan."

Finding 01 — The Planner Tax

In every group, one person consistently ends up synthesizing everyone's links and preferences into a plan. The rest of the group "engages" without doing any coordination work.

"I just stop replying in the group chat because I know if I engage, I become the planner. And I'm always the planner."
Finding 02 — Links Are Shared Across Platforms

In a single planning conversation, links come from Instagram, Zomato, Google Maps, and BookMyShow — often in the same thread. Nobody cross-references them.

"I have like four tabs open and I'm trying to figure out if these places are even near each other. It's exhausting."
Finding 03 — Too Many Options = No Decision

When the group surfaced more than three or four ideas, the probability of a plan actually happening dropped significantly. More options created more discussion — not more decisions.

"We had six places shortlisted and then just... nothing happened. Too many opinions."
Finding 04 — The Default Fallback

When planning failed, groups defaulted to a place they'd been before — not because it was best, but because it required zero decision-making effort.

Finding 05 — Sharing is Commitment

When someone shared a formatted plan in the chat, others responded and committed. The act of sharing something concrete — not just a link — was the trigger for group buy-in.

"Once someone actually writes it out with times, it becomes real. Before that it's just vibes."

4. The Solution

PlanDump AI takes the raw material of group chat planning — links, screenshots, even typed notes — and does all the coordination work automatically. The output is exactly three itinerary options, formatted to share directly to WhatsApp.

Input: Paste any combination of public URLs (Zomato, Instagram, Google Maps, BookMyShow), screenshots, or place names. No login required.

Output: Three distinct, ready-to-share plans — each optimized for a different group priority.

Path A

High-Efficiency

Sequenced to minimize travel time and traffic. Best for groups that want to cover the most ground.

Path B

Low-Stress

Wider time buffers, fewer transitions, prioritizes casual spots with no reservations required.

Path C

Budget Saver

Free and low-cost options sequenced around walkable corridors or public transit.

Three options is a deliberate constraint. The research showed that more options produce more discussion and fewer decisions. Three is enough to feel like choice; few enough to produce a winner.

5. System Design

The backend runs a three-stage pipeline designed around one principle: extract meaning from chaos, then reduce it to a decision.

[ Raw Input: Links, screenshots, place names ] │ ▼ ┌─────────────────────────────┐ │ UNDERSTAND │ │ Extract structured data │──► name, coordinates, │ from unstructured input │ hours, cost, vibe tags └─────────────────┬───────────┘ │ ▼ ┌─────────────────────────────┐ │ OPTIMIZE │ │ Group locations by │──► Ordered sequence │ geography, time, cost │ for each path type └─────────────────┬───────────┘ │ ▼ ┌─────────────────────────────┐ │ RECOMMEND │ │ Apply constraints and │──► 3 shareable │ format output │ itinerary cards └─────────────────────────────┘

Fallback: When Scraping Fails

Platforms like Instagram restrict automated scraping. If the Understand stage hits a block, the UI immediately prompts the user to type the place name or paste a screenshot. The system switches to semantic search lookup — no broken experience, no dead end.

6. Product Decisions & Tradeoffs

Three paths, not a customizable dashboard

The temptation is to give users filters: sort by distance, toggle budget, adjust time. But filters recreate the original problem — they require the user to make more decisions. Three pre-optimized paths eliminate that friction entirely.

Voting, not collaborative editing

PlanDump doesn't need a shared planning canvas or a comment thread. It needs a decision. Adding a simple vote (👍 on Path A, B, or C) with a 30-minute auto-close converts "what should we do?" into "we're doing this." Without voting, the product solves half the problem.

No login required (MVP constraint)

Requiring account creation before generating a plan adds friction at exactly the wrong moment. The MVP generates plans without login; sharing and saving can prompt optional sign-up as a natural next step.

PlanDump lives inside the WhatsApp workflow, not outside it

The product doesn't compete with group chats — it plugs into them. The user brings chaos from WhatsApp, gets a plan, and takes it back to WhatsApp. The share button is the core action, not a nice-to-have.

8. Open Risks & Mitigations

Scraping limitations from major platforms

Mitigation: Screenshot and manual input fallback with identical UX flow. Scope clearly in the MVP — links are supported, screenshots and typed notes ship in v2.

Plans generated for the wrong city or context

Mitigation: The parser extracts coordinates from structured URLs where available; location is surfaced as a confirmable field before output is generated.

The adoption gap: why not just decide in WhatsApp?

Mitigation: PlanDump doesn't ask groups to change their behavior — it inserts into the moment when WhatsApp has already failed them. The trigger is decision fatigue, not discovery.

One person uses it; the group doesn't

Mitigation: The shared itinerary card is the distribution mechanism. The planner shares a link; the group votes without ever opening the app. Viral loop is built into the core action.

10. What I'd Validate First

Core assumption: Groups abandon plans because synthesizing options into a concrete itinerary takes more effort than the plan is worth.

Experiment: Run PlanDump with 10 friend groups over two weekends. Measure whether having a generated plan increases follow-through compared to unassisted planning.

Signal I'm watching: Not time saved — but whether the plan actually happened. A 4-minute planning session that produces a real Saturday is more valuable than an 18-minute one that doesn't.
